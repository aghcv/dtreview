\subsection{Conceptual definitions and the digital patient spectrum}

The included reviews used digital twin terminology alongside patient digital twin, human digital twin, virtual patient, in silico patient or trial, digital patient, synthetic patient, and adjacent constructs. Digital twin was the primary classified term in 47 records, while virtual patient, human digital twin, patient digital twin, and in silico patient or trial were less frequent (Table~\ref{tab:concepts}).


The operational attributes emphasized in the National Academies definition were not uniformly co-reported.\cite{nasem2024digitaltwins} Dynamic or continuous updating language appeared in 54 records and bidirectional coupling in 33, while 39 records described one-way coupling and 15 described explicitly static constructs. These non-mutually exclusive patterns show that patient specificity, biological hierarchy, temporality, and coupling direction varied independently. Table~\ref{tab:concepts} summarizes the individual attributes. Figure~\ref{fig:spectrum} maps their combinations across the included reviews. The resulting pattern supports a multidimensional spectrum that cannot be reduced to terminology alone. The spectrum describes how the reviews characterized models; it does not assess whether any individual model satisfies the National Academies definition.

\subsection{Modeling and computational pipeline}

Construction approaches were multimodal and heterogeneous. Imaging was reported in 49 records, wearables or sensor data in 43, EHR or clinical-record data in 29, and omics or genomics in 24. AI or machine-learning approaches appeared in 50 records, mechanistic or physics-based models in 38, and hybrid approaches in 33. Digital patient construction was therefore usually described as a combination of data-driven and mechanistic components rather than a single model class. Table~\ref{tab:pipeline} gives the underlying counts. Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline} shows how data inputs, model families, and clinical or research outputs relate within the computational pipeline.

Named interoperability standards were reported less consistently than multimodal inputs or model families. DICOM appeared in 15 records, HL7 in 14, FHIR in 13, and OMOP in 11; no ontology or identifier category was detected by the current classification rules. These counts show that reviews were aware of named standards, but they do not demonstrate implementation conformance, semantic interoperability, or end-to-end data provenance.

\subsection{Validation, fidelity, and clinical maturity}

Validation reporting ranged from conceptual discussion to empirical patient-data evaluation, simulation or benchmark comparison, cross-validation, and limited prospective evidence. Empirical or patient-data validation was mentioned in 25 records, simulation or benchmark comparison in 18, sensitivity or uncertainty analysis in 13, and cross-validation or holdout testing in eight. Six records were classified as conceptual or without model evaluation, and three mentioned expert evaluation. Table~\ref{tab:validation} provides the detailed counts. Figure~\ref{fig:validation} contrasts the reported validation approaches with model maturity.

Reported maturity did not map directly to the strength of validation evidence. Prototype or proof-of-concept language appeared in 43 records and theoretical or conceptual language in 21. Although deployed or operational language appeared in 19 records, only three records mentioned prospective or clinical-study evidence and one mentioned retrospective validation. Because maturity and validation categories are non-mutually exclusive review-level signals, this contrast does not establish that any specific deployed model lacked validation; it shows that operational claims were more commonly reported than the stronger study designs used to substantiate them.

The extraction captured validation method, data source, fidelity criteria, uncertainty, reproducibility, and maturity, but did not directly code a formal question of interest, context-of-use statement, model influence, or decision consequence. The standards-informed comparison is therefore a description of reporting coverage, not an assessment of regulatory adequacy under the FDA or ASME frameworks.\cite{fda2023credibility,asme2018vv40}

\subsection{Applications}

Applications spanned multiple clinical and research domains. Cardiovascular applications were the most frequently matched domain (32/64, 50.0\%), linking multimodal patient phenotyping and physiologic or hemodynamic models to risk assessment, procedural planning, and treatment selection. Oncology was represented in 25 records (39.1\%), where reviews commonly combined imaging, molecular or multi-omics data, and virtual cohorts to support tumor detection, prognosis, treatment-response modeling, and therapy optimization. Surgery, orthopedics, and dentistry appeared in 24 records (37.5\%), spanning image-guided and robotic procedures, arthroscopic soft-tissue simulation, prosthodontic or dental virtual patients, preoperative rehearsal, and postoperative assessment. Neurology and neuroscience were matched in 18 records (28.1\%), including brain and spine surgery, headache and migraine, stroke, multiple sclerosis, and rehabilitation applications that paired neuroimaging or physiologic data with diagnostic and decision-support models. Diabetes and endocrine applications were matched in 17 records (26.6\%); these reviews emphasized glucose--insulin dynamics, metabolic risk, chronic-disease monitoring, and simulation of individualized responses to diet, activity, medication, or insulin therapy.

Treatment or therapy planning was the leading functional category (56/64, 87.5\%), reflecting the use of virtual models to compare interventions and refine surgical, rehabilitation, medication, or dosing strategies before they are applied to patients. Prediction, prognosis, or risk assessment was matched in 49 records (76.6\%), most often as forecasting disease trajectories, complications, treatment response, or patient-level risk from longitudinal and multimodal inputs. Diagnosis or detection appeared in 46 records (71.9\%), including image-based classification, physiologic-signal interpretation, anomaly detection, early screening, and diagnostic decision support. Education or training was identified in 19 records (29.7\%), where virtual patients and simulations supported clinical reasoning, procedural rehearsal, technical-skills training, or patient education. Personalized care was explicitly coded as a function in nine records (14.1\%); this lower count does not imply that personalization was absent, because many reviews treated patient specificity as a model property or anticipated benefit rather than as a distinct application category. Table~\ref{tab:applications} summarizes the domain and function counts. Since a review could address more than one domain or function, Figure~\ref{fig:applications} presents their co-occurrence instead of assigning each review to a single category.

\subsection{Barriers and future directions}

Barriers were reported across nearly every layer of the proposed digital patient ecosystem. Privacy, security, and governance were matched in 51 records (79.7\%), reflecting the difficulty of protecting continuously assembled longitudinal, imaging, sensor, genomic, and clinical data while clarifying consent, ownership, access control, and stewardship. Ethical, legal, and regulatory issues were also matched in 51 records (79.7\%); recurring concerns included bias and representativeness, explainability and transparency, responsibility for model-informed decisions, liability, and the absence of clear regulatory pathways. Computing, scalability, and resource constraints appeared in 51 records (79.7\%), because high-fidelity multiscale simulation, AI training, real-time updating, and clinical deployment impose substantial demands on processing, latency, cost, infrastructure, and technical expertise. Interoperability and standards were matched in 49 records (76.6\%), underscoring persistent difficulty harmonizing EHR, imaging, laboratory, omics, and wearable data across platforms even when standards such as FHIR, HL7, and DICOM were named. Clinical adoption and workflow and data quality, availability, and integration each appeared in 40 records, while validation, evidence, and reproducibility appeared in 34. Table~\ref{tab:barriers} lists the barrier counts. Figure~\ref{fig:barriers} shows their relative frequency. All domain, function, and barrier counts are record-level, rule-based, and non-mutually exclusive; they indicate the presence of a coded theme rather than its evidentiary strength or independence.

Broad interoperability or standardization concerns were reported more often than any named implementation standard, a difference evident when the pipeline and barrier results are compared (Tables~\ref{tab:pipeline} and~\ref{tab:barriers}). Validation and reproducibility were also recurrent barriers, despite less consistent reporting of detailed fidelity metrics and uncertainty analyses. The validation counts are reported in Table~\ref{tab:validation}. Figure~\ref{fig:validation} shows the balance of validation approaches and maturity levels. The principal gaps therefore lie at the interfaces between model construction, evidence generation, data exchange, and clinical use, rather than within any one technology.

The corresponding priorities in Table~\ref{tab:recommendations} emphasize standardized terminology, explicit maturity levels, stronger validation, interoperable infrastructure, reproducible supplements, workflow and governance evaluation, and prospective evidence. These recommendations summarize what the reviews proposed; they should not be interpreted as an existing consensus standard.
